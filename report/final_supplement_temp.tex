\section{Seasonal trend structure}

Sen's slope magnitudes for all four variables at annual and seasonal resolution are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:s_trendheatmap}. The heatmap complements Table~2 of the main text by exposing the seasonal breakdown of the warming signal and the seasonal structure of Italian evapotranspiration increase. Italy's $ET$ increase is concentrated in spring (MAM, $+2.0$~mm~yr$^{-1}$) and summer (JJA, $+1.4$~mm~yr$^{-1}$); Bangladesh's precipitation decline is distributed across all seasons but is strongest in winter (DJF) and autumn (SON). Saudi warming is largest in autumn and winter, a pattern consistent with enhanced night-time temperature increases in arid regions.

\end{figure}

\section{Full annual SHAP feature distributions}

The bar chart in Fig.~5 of the main text summarises SHAP contributions by their mean absolute value, but hides the direction and spread of individual contributions. Figure~\ref{fig:s_beeswarm} shows the full SHAP distribution for every feature using beeswarm plots. The direction encoding confirms that high precipitation values consistently drive positive SHAP contributions (red points on the positive axis), while high soil temperature values contribute negatively to predicted runoff in Italy (red points on the negative axis), consistent with evapotranspiration suppression under warm conditions.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig11b_annual_shap_summary.png}
    \caption{Annual SHAP beeswarm plots. Each point is one pixel-year from the test set. Horizontal position: SHAP contribution to predicted runoff; colour: raw feature value (red high, blue low).}
    \label{fig:s_beeswarm}
\end{figure}

\section{Temporal evolution of SHAP attributions}

Figure~\ref{fig:s_temporalshap} shows how the mean annual SHAP contribution of each feature has evolved between 1950 and 2025. Year-to-year variability is dominated by precipitation, particularly in Bangladesh, where the 1970s to 1990s show repeated high-SHAP excursions coinciding with strong monsoon years. In Italy, a mild declining trend in the precipitation SHAP contribution during the post-2000 period is consistent with the parallel increase in evapotranspiration documented in the main text (Section~4.2). Saudi Arabia shows no coherent temporal structure in any feature beyond the first decade, consistent with the low overall predictability of the arid-zone model.

\section{Monthly-scale models: justifying the annual focus}

In addition to the annual model used in the main text, we also trained parallel monthly XGBoost models on pixel-month records. Their performance and SHAP rankings motivate why we chose the annual model for physical interpretation in the main text.

The monthly model performs markedly worse than the annual model in the hyper-arid regime ($R^2 = 0.36$ vs.\ 0.59 for Saudi Arabia). It also exhibits a seasonality-driven attribution anomaly: in Italy the monthly model ranks soil temperature as the top feature (mean $|\mathrm{SHAP}|$ $\sim\!56$~mm~month$^{-1}$), well above precipitation ($\sim\!16$~mm~month$^{-1}$). We interpret monthly soil temperature as a seasonal proxy --- low winter values coincide with high runoff from snowmelt and low evapotranspiration, while high summer values coincide with suppressed runoff --- and the model exploits this seasonal covariance as a predictive shortcut. Aggregation to annual resolution removes this within-year covariance and places precipitation unambiguously first in all three countries. Monthly SHAP results are therefore informative about the model's internal structure but should not be used as a basis for physical attribution, which is why the main text reports only annual results.
